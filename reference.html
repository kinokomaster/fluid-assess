1. The Foundation — Two Questions

Module 1

Volume assessment requires answering two independent questions:

Question 1 — Is the patient fluid responsive?

Will giving fluid actually increase cardiac output? → Answered by IVC.

Question 2 — Is the patient fluid tolerant?

Can fluid be given without causing organ congestion? → Answered by VExUS.

A patient can be one but not the other. Both must be answered before deciding to give, hold, or remove fluid.

2. IVC — How to Obtain

Module 2
  • Probe: Cardiac (phased-array)
  • Window: Subxiphoid — slightly right of midline, rock cephalad
  • Identify the IVC as it enters the right atrium
  • Drop M-mode through the IVC ~1–2 cm from the RA junction to capture max/min diameters across the respiratory cycle

Examples

Collapsed IVC — suggests fluid responsiveness
Plethoric IVC — suggests high RA pressure / fluid intolerance

3. IVC — How to Interpret

Module 2

The evidence-synthesized bedside rule:

  • Fill if IVC < 1 cm
  • Don't fill if IVC > 2.5 cm
  • IVC 1–2.5 cm → assess collapsibility (ΔIVC):
    • Spontaneously breathing: ≥ 35% (≈ 1/3) → likely responsive
    • Mechanically ventilated: ≥ 16.5% (≈ 1/5) → likely responsive

In mechanically ventilated patients, ideal interpretation requires tidal volume ≤ 8 mL/kg and PEEP ≤ 5 cmH₂O.

Evidence base

  • Barbier 2004 (Intensive Care Med 30:1834-7) — Ventilated septic shock; ΔIVC ≥ 12% → Positive Predictive Value 92%, Negative Predictive Value 93%.
  • Muller 2012 (Crit Care 16:R188) — Spontaneously breathing shock; ΔIVC ≥ 40% → Sensitivity 70%, Specificity 80%.
  • ICM Editorial 2024 (Intensive Care Med 50:1137-42) — Practical bedside rules synthesized.
  • Meta-analysis 2025 (PLoS One 20(5):e0310462) — 21 studies, sepsis. Overall Sensitivity 0.84 / Specificity 0.87.

4. IVC — ASE 2025 RA Pressure Update

Module 2

The classic ASE framework placed RA pressure into three categories using IVC diameter (cutoff 2.1 cm) and collapsibility (cutoff 50%):

  • Low — IVC ≤ 2.1 cm + collapsibility > 50% → ~3 mmHg (range 0–5)
  • Intermediate — one criterion met → ~8 mmHg (range 5–10)
  • High — IVC > 2.1 cm + collapsibility < 50% → ~15 mmHg (range 10–20)

The ASE 2025 update revises this approach to improve accuracy in indeterminate cases by incorporating additional sonographic signs. No need to memorize — save the image as a reference.

For volume assessment, the cutoffs above (Section 3) remain the practical bedside rule.

5. VExUS — Three Vessels & S/D Waves

Module 3

VExUS = Venous Excess UltraSound. The more congested the venous system, the more back-flow appears in the veins. Doppler waveforms capture this back-flow across three vessels:

  • ① Hepatic vein — pressure transmission from the RA during systole and diastole. First to change as RA pressure rises.
  • ② Portal vein — sinusoidal congestion in the liver. Becomes pulsatile as upstream congestion worsens.
  • ③ Renal interlobar vein — venous outflow obstruction in the kidney. Closest to organ injury (AKI). Hardest to obtain.

Prerequisite: VExUS is intended for patients with evidence of congestion — typically IVC > 2 cm. In patients without IVC dilation, VExUS adds little.

S wave / D wave refresher

Both waves represent forward flow toward the right atrium:

  • S wave — forward flow during systole (RV contraction; tricuspid annulus moves toward apex)
  • D wave — forward flow during diastole (RV relaxes and passively fills)

As RA pressure rises, the S wave shrinks first (because the tricuspid annulus motion is dampened). The D wave is more robust.

6. VExUS — Hepatic Vein

Module 3

How to obtain

  1. Use a cardiac (phased-array) probe or an abdominal (curvilinear) probe
  2. Cardiac probe → subxiphoid window. Curvilinear → right mid-axillary
  3. Identify a hepatic vein entering the IVC
  4. Place the sample gate on the hepatic vein and apply pulsed-wave (PW) Doppler
Curvilinear probe approach from right mid-axillary (starts at 2:10)

Three patterns

Normal
S > D
Normal RA pressure; S wave dominates.
Mild congestion
S < D
S wave attenuated as RA pressure rises.
Severe congestion
S reversal
S wave inverted below baseline.

How to read it: Compare S and D wave amplitudes at end-expiration.

Reference: Nephropocus

7. VExUS — Portal Vein

Module 3

How to obtain

  1. Use an abdominal (curvilinear) probe
  2. Approach from the right mid-axillary window
  3. Starting from the hepatic vein view, tilt the probe slightly anterior (ventral)
  4. Identify a portal vein — color Doppler highlights flow into the liver
  5. Place the sample gate on the portal vein and apply PW Doppler
Portal vein approach starts at 4:50

Three patterns — Pulsatility Index (PI)

PI = (Vmax − Vmin) ÷ Vmax × 100%

Normal
PI < 30%
Near-continuous flow; sinusoids dampen pulsations.
Mild
PI 30–50%
Pulsatility appears as dampening fails.
Severe
PI ≥ 50%
May show flow reversal at troughs.

Reference: Nephropocus

8. VExUS — Renal Interlobar Vein

Module 3
📌 Optional vessel

The renal interlobar vein is the hardest of the three to obtain. mVExUS (Section 10) omits this vessel with comparable performance — consider it if the renal view is difficult.

How to obtain

  1. Use an abdominal (curvilinear) probe
  2. Approach from the right or left mid-axillary window
  3. Identify the kidney parenchyma
  4. Apply color Doppler to find intra-renal vessels
  5. Place the sample gate on an intra-renal vessel and apply PW Doppler
Renal interlobar vein approach starts at 6:40

Three patterns

Normal
Continuous
Smooth, continuous flow.
Mild
Biphasic
Distinct S and D components.
Severe
Monophasic
Only the D component remains.

9. VExUS Grading 0–3

Module 3

Combines IVC dilation (gate) with the worst Doppler pattern among the three vessels:

  • Grade 0 — No congestion: IVC < 2 cm or all vessels normal
  • Grade 1 — Mild: IVC ≥ 2 cm + at least one mildly abnormal vessel; no severe findings
  • Grade 2 — Moderate: IVC ≥ 2 cm + at least one vessel with a severe pattern
  • Grade 3 — Severe: IVC ≥ 2 cm + ≥ 2 vessels with severe patterns

Two principles

  1. IVC < 2 cm → Grade 0 regardless of vessel patterns.
  2. The worst vessel wins — single severe finding bumps the patient up.

Outcome data (Beaubien-Souligny 2020 — post-cardiac surgery, ventilated)

  • Grade 1 → AKI Hazard Ratio 1.25
  • Grade 2 → AKI Hazard Ratio 2.65
  • Grade 3 → AKI Hazard Ratio 3.69

Clinical takeaway: Grade ≥ 2 suggests a potential need for diuresis.

⚖️ Evidence caveat

Best validated in cardiac / perioperative settings. A 2025 follow-up study (Ann Intensive Care 2025;15:105) found no association with AKI or mortality in septic ICU patients. Interpret VExUS in clinical context — population matters.

10. mVExUS — Modified Protocol

Module 3

Modified VExUS (mVExUS) drops the renal interlobar vein, leaving only hepatic + portal. Evidence (Ultrasound Journal 2025;17:7) suggests mVExUS performs comparably to full VExUS in detecting elevated RA pressure.

  • 2 venous Doppler windows instead of 3 — easier and faster at the bedside
  • Renal interlobar vein is the hardest of the three to obtain reliably
  • Full VExUS remains the validated reference for grading

When to use mVExUS: When the renal view is technically difficult or time is short.

11. Clinical Application — The Decision Matrix

Modules 1 & 3 · Cases 1–3

Practical workflow

  1. Start with IVC — assess fluid responsiveness
  2. If IVC > 2 cm → proceed to VExUS to assess tolerance
  3. At the bedside, use mVExUS (hepatic + portal)
  4. Synthesize → fluid, hold, diurese, or pressors

When both static tools are ambiguous → dynamic testing

If IVC is unreliable (e.g., high PEEP) and VExUS is only mildly abnormal (Grade 1), neither static tool gives a clean answer. A small fluid challenge (250–500 mL) with reassessment in 30–60 min reveals both responsiveness and tolerance — the patient's response is the most informative test.

12. Caveats & Limitations

Modules 2 & 3
⚠️ IVC is unreliable at high PEEP

IVC distensibility becomes unreliable when PEEP > 5 cmH₂O — the exaggerated respiratory swing no longer reflects true preload responsiveness. Reliable interpretation also requires tidal volume ≤ 8 mL/kg. In high-PEEP settings, lean on VExUS or dynamic testing.

⚠️ Pressure ≠ Volume

A dilated IVC reflects high RA pressure — which can come from RV dysfunction, tension pneumothorax, or cardiac tamponade, not just volume overload. Rule these out before trusting IVC alone.

⚠️ VExUS evidence is population-dependent

Best validated in cardiac surgery / perioperative populations. A 2025 follow-up in septic ICU patients did not replicate the AKI/mortality association. Use VExUS as one input — not the sole driver — of management decisions.

⚠️ Grade 1 is ambiguous

Grade 1 (mild) findings alone don't mandate diuresis. When VExUS is borderline and IVC is unreliable, a small dynamic fluid challenge reveals the truth.
