📊 Module 1 — Volume Assessment
📊 Introduction

What is Volume Assessment?

When a patient is in shock or has borderline urine output, we need to decide: give more fluid, start pressors, or diurese? Volume assessment helps us answer that question — systematically.

At its core, volume assessment requires answering two separate questions:

Question 1
Is this patient fluid responsive?
Will giving fluid actually increase cardiac output?
Question 2
Is this patient fluid tolerant?
Can we give fluid safely, without causing harm or organ congestion?
💡 Key Insight

These are two independent questions. A patient can be fluid responsive but NOT fluid tolerant — or vice versa. Answering only one is not enough.

💧 Fluid Responsiveness

Will fluid increase cardiac output?

Fluid responsiveness tells us whether the heart is on the steep portion of the Frank-Starling curve — where adding volume translates into more output.

Definition
Fluid Responsiveness
The ability of the heart to increase cardiac output in response to a fluid challenge.
📐 Clinical Definition

Fluid responsiveness is defined as a 10–15% increase in cardiac output following a 250–500 mL fluid bolus, or following passive leg raising (PLR).

Important to remember

Being fluid responsive does not automatically mean you should give fluid. You must also assess whether the patient can tolerate it.

🛡️ Fluid Tolerance

Can fluid be given without harm?

Even if a patient would respond to fluid, congestion may already be present — and adding more could worsen organ injury.

Definition
Fluid Tolerance
The ability to receive fluid without developing harmful venous congestion or organ injury.
Fluid Tolerant ✓
No significant venous congestion. Fluid can be given safely with expected benefit.
Not Fluid Tolerant ✗
Venous congestion present. Additional fluid risks worsening AKI, gut edema, or respiratory failure.
⚠️ Clinical Pearl

Fluid intolerance is especially important to recognize in patients with heart failure, ARDS, or those who have already received large volumes.

🔬 Assessment Framework

How Do We Assess Volume Status?

Bedside ultrasound provides multiple windows into volume status. No single view tells the whole story — a systematic approach is key.

① IVC
Size & collapsibility
② Extravascular
Lung B-lines, ascites
③ Left Heart
LV function & filling
④ Right Heart
RV function & pressure
⑤ VExUS
Venous congestion grading
⚖️ The Key Takeaway

Two Tools, Two Questions

IVC and VExUS are complementary — together they give a complete picture of whether to give fluid, hold it, or actively remove it.

Question 1 of 4
Volume assessment involves two key components. Which pair correctly represents these two components?
✅ Correct — B. Volume assessment requires answering two distinct questions: (1) Is the patient fluid responsive — will cardiac output increase? And (2) Is the patient fluid tolerant — can fluid be given without causing congestion or organ harm?
✏️ Knowledge Check
Question 2 of 4
True or False: IVC evaluation alone is sufficient to guide all fluid management decisions.
✅ Correct — B. IVC primarily evaluates fluid responsiveness. While a markedly dilated IVC (e.g., >25mm with minimal collapsibility) can hint at intolerance, VExUS is the primary tool for comprehensively assessing venous congestion and fluid tolerance.
✏️ Knowledge Check
Question 3 of 4
Match the tool to its primary purpose.

IVC evaluation is most useful for assessing fluid ___, while VExUS evaluation is most useful for assessing fluid ___.
✅ Correct — B. IVC size and collapsibility primarily inform us about fluid responsiveness (will cardiac output increase?). VExUS Doppler patterns in the hepatic, portal, and renal veins inform us about fluid tolerance (is there venous congestion?).
✏️ Knowledge Check
Question 4 of 4
A 68-year-old woman presents with septic shock. You want to know whether a fluid bolus will increase her cardiac output. Which bedside ultrasound assessment should you prioritize first?
✅ Correct — B. The question asks specifically about fluid responsiveness — will a bolus increase cardiac output? IVC evaluation (size and collapsibility) is the primary bedside tool for this. VExUS would be used alongside or after to assess fluid tolerance.

📋 Module Summary

Key Concepts — Module 1

A quick-reference recap of everything covered in this module.

💧 Fluid Responsiveness
  • Will cardiac output increase with fluid?
  • Defined as ≥10–15% CO increase with bolus or PLR
  • Primary tool: IVC evaluation
  • Responsive ≠ safe to give fluid
🛡️ Fluid Tolerance
  • Can fluid be given without causing harm?
  • Assessed by looking for venous congestion
  • Primary tool: VExUS grading
  • Intolerance → risk of AKI, pulmonary edema
