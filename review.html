Putting It All Together

A quick recap of the three modules before we apply the framework to clinical cases.

What you've learned

Module 1
Volume Assessment
  • Volume assessment = responsiveness + tolerance
  • A patient can be one but not the other
  • You need both questions answered to make a safe fluid decision
Module 2
IVC → Responsiveness
  • Fill if IVC < 1 cm; don't fill if > 2.5 cm
  • IVC 1–2.5 cm → assess collapsibility (≥ 35% spontaneous, ≥ 16.5% ventilated)
  • Pressure ≠ volume — rule out RV failure, tamponade, PTX
Module 3
VExUS → Tolerance
  • Hepatic + portal + renal vein Doppler → Grade 0–3
  • mVExUS (hepatic + portal only) for bedside use
  • Grade ≥ 2 → consider diuresis

The two questions to answer

Question 1
Is the patient fluid responsive?

Will giving fluid actually increase cardiac output?

Tool
IVC size + collapsibility
  • Small / collapsing → responsive
  • Dilated / non-collapsing → not responsive
Question 2
Is the patient fluid tolerant?

Can fluid be given without causing harm (organ congestion)?

Tool
VExUS (hepatic, portal, renal Doppler)
  • Grade 0–1 → tolerant
  • Grade ≥ 2 → not tolerant

These are two independent questions. A patient can be responsive but not tolerant — or vice versa. Both must be answered before deciding to give, hold, or remove fluid.

⚠️ IVC Caveats — interpret with caution

  • High PEEP (> 5 cmH₂O): IVC distensibility becomes unreliable — the exaggerated respiratory swing no longer faithfully reflects preload responsiveness. Lean on VExUS or dynamic testing in this setting.
  • Pressure ≠ Volume: A dilated IVC reflects high RA pressure, which can come from RV dysfunction, tension pneumothorax, or cardiac tamponade — not just volume overload. Rule these out before trusting IVC alone.
